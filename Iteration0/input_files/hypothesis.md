The current research focuses on the linear interaction between policy variables and capital accumulation, but it overlooks the non-linear "catch-up" threshold inherent in the Cobb-Douglas production function. I hypothesize that the impact of `trade_openness` and `government_expenditure_share` on growth is not constant, but conditional on the country's distance from the global TFP frontier. Specifically, I propose a Threshold Panel Regression model to test if policy effectiveness (the marginal effect of $s_t$ on $\Delta \ln Y_t$) undergoes a structural break once a country reaches a specific percentile of the TFP distribution. By estimating a threshold model where the transition variable is the relative TFP gap ($\ln A_{i,t} - \ln A_{max,t}$), we can determine if trade openness acts as a "catalyst" for growth only for countries lagging behind the frontier, while potentially showing diminishing returns for those near the steady-state. This approach moves beyond simple interaction terms to identify the "developmental window" where specific macro-policies are most effective, providing a more nuanced understanding of the transition dynamics than the current linear GMM approach.
