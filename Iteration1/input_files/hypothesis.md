Title: Identifying Non-Linear TFP Catch-up Thresholds via Threshold Vector Autoregression (TVAR)

Hypothesis: The previous research focused on linear interactions between policy variables and capital accumulation, assuming a constant impact of trade and government spending. However, the structural model's TFP dynamics suggest that countries may experience "productivity traps" or "take-off" points. I hypothesize that the impact of `trade_openness` and `human_capital` on GDP growth is non-linear and conditional on the country's relative distance to the global TFP frontier. Specifically, I propose that there exists a threshold level of `human_capital` below which `trade_openness` has a negligible or negative effect on growth, and above which it acts as a significant catalyst for TFP convergence. 

To test this, we will implement a Threshold Panel Regression model where the transition variable is the country's TFP gap relative to the global maximum. By estimating the threshold parameter endogenously, we can identify whether policy effectiveness is regime-dependent. This moves beyond the previous linear GMM approach by explicitly modeling the structural heterogeneity in how countries respond to policy levers based on their current stage of technological development, rather than assuming a uniform marginal effect across the entire panel.
