The "catch-up" speed of developing economies is governed by a threshold effect in human capital, where the marginal return of trade openness on capital accumulation is non-linear. I hypothesize that the interaction between `trade_openness` and `human_capital` exhibits a "tipping point" (a structural break in the production function's transition dynamics). Specifically, below a critical threshold of `human_capital`, trade openness fails to accelerate capital deepening due to an inability to absorb foreign technology, whereas above this threshold, the interaction term becomes strongly positive and significant. To test this, I propose implementing a Panel Threshold Regression (PTR) model that endogenously estimates the threshold value of `human_capital` and compares the transition velocity of capital stock ($K_{t+1}$) across regimes. This moves beyond the previous linear interaction models by allowing the structural impact of policy variables to switch states based on the country's development level, providing a more nuanced explanation for why government expenditure and trade openness have shown inconsistent significance in previous iterations.
